A777ance — 90-Day Marketing Strategy: Western MA & Connecticut

Drafted 2026-07-14, pricing corrected 2026-07-15. Executes the Phase 1 roadmap already in MARKETING/README.md ("liquidity before app, trust before tech") against a specific geography and a real prospect list. Pricing follows the Master Amounts Calculator (claude/master-amounts-calculator-okqphz, pricing/master-amounts-calculator.html) — confirmed authoritative, superseding the README's older ADR-007 "$175 + $35/mo standard" figure and the "$29/mo founding" promo, which the calculator doesn't model. Only the $50/mo flat operator due (ADR-005) matches across both sources.

1. Why this geography

Two clusters, chosen for route density, not sprawl — the "zip-code-at-a-time" rule in the existing roadmap means scattered homes exhaust an operator, so the 90-day radius should be small enough to drive in one afternoon:

  • Anchor cluster — Pioneer Valley, Western MA: Northampton, Amherst, Hadley, Easthampton, Florence, South Hadley, West Springfield, Springfield.
  • Adjacent cluster — North-Central CT: Enfield, Suffield, Windsor Locks, Simsbury, Granby — a 20–30 min drive south of Northampton down I-91, so one operator route can plausibly cover both sides of the state line without a second hire.

Don't try to cover all of Western MA + all of CT in 90 days. Pick one town in each cluster to go deep in first (recommend Northampton, MA + Enfield or Simsbury, CT), then expand ring-by-ring only once density is proven there.

2. The 90-day plan (phased, per the existing roadmap)

Days 1–30 — Proof + positioning

  • Turn the existing 3 clients into case studies. Named or anonymized testimonials ("Jose patched my fridge while I slept"). This is the single highest-leverage asset you have and it's unused — do it before any outreach.
  • Own "pest control for your network" in local content: 2–3 short posts/pages answering "is your smart TV spying on you?" / "why grandma's tablet is a target" — written for a Northampton/Enfield reader, not a general audience.
  • Pick the lead town per cluster (see §1) and map every household you can reasonably reach by referral or door route in that town — this is your Phase 1 density target, not "Western MA" as a whole.
  • Draft outreach scripts per channel type (see §4) — one for HOA/property managers, one for real-estate agents, one for elder-care agencies, one for potential operator recruits (local IT/computer-repair techs).

Days 31–60 — Channel partner outreach + operator recruiting

  • Call the shortlist in §3, lead-town entries first. Goal: 2–3 warm conversations per channel type, not mass outreach — this is a trust business, not a lead-gen funnel.
  • Recruit 1–2 non-founder operators. The strongest candidates are local computer-repair/IT technicians who already do in-home visits (§3.4) — they have the skill and the truck; you're offering them a guild + a flat retainer model instead of one-off tickets.
  • Join one chamber of commerce per cluster (Greater Northampton Chamber, and the Granby-Simsbury Chamber for CT) — not for the badge, but for the monthly networking night, where HOA managers, real-estate agents, and eldercare operators are already in the room together.
  • Make the referral CTA live inside the next statement cycle for the existing 3 clients — "refer a neighbor" — so the first channel-partner conversations aren't your only source of new homes.

Days 61–90 — Convert + measure

  • Close whichever channel relationship is warmest into a pilot: a real-estate agent offering A777ance setup as a move-in add-on, an HOA newsletter mention, or an elder-care agency co-referral (their aides notice grandma's network is a mess; you fix it).
  • Report against the metrics in §5 and decide, per the roadmap's own gate, whether you're at "10–20 homes + 1–2 operators" — the trigger for Phase 2 (the unified toggle app). If not, extend Phase 1 rather than building early.

CUSTOMER CHANNEL

3.1 HOA / condo & property management — the "one neighborhood" channel

Why it works: a property manager already sends a monthly newsletter and sits in every board meeting; recommending a vendor costs them nothing and looks like a favor to residents. Value to them: fewer "my Wi-Fi is broken" calls routed to their office, plus a liability angle worth naming explicitly — a compromised resident network or shared-building device is a risk the manager absorbs too, not just an inconvenience. Value to A777ance: every home in one association is on the same street — the exact route-density Phase 1 needs.

The technical claim behind that pitch (verifiable, not marketing copy): per this stack's own localDNS/CLAUDE.md, personal/sensitive DNS queries resolve recursively through Unbound with DNSSEC and never leave the house; only bulk, low-sensitivity streaming domains get forwarded, encrypted, to Cloudflare for speed. "Residents' data doesn't route through a third-party cloud" is accurate for this specific setup — usable phrasing for a liability-conscious property manager or, per §3.6 below, an insurance agent.

Sizing the opportunity (illustrative): condo/HOA communities nationally average ~77 units (28.2M units ÷ ~365,000 associations — iPropertyManagement, DoorLoop), but most local, non-tower associations run smaller — "dozens to a few hundred" per the same sources. For a single Western MA/CT association, 25–75 units is a more realistic working range than the national average. Layer the flat 5% conversion on top:

3.2 Real-estate brokerages — the "new homeowner" channel

Why it works: move-in week is the single highest-intent moment for a household to accept a network setup offer, and it costs the agent nothing to mention. Value to them: a free differentiator to hand buyers ("one less thing on your list"). Value to A777ance: a warm intro timed to exactly when someone's setting up a new home.

Sizing the opportunity (illustrative): NAR reports a median of 10 transaction sides per agent in 2024 (12 in the 2022 member profile — HomeLight, Leanprop); treating that as roughly half buyer-side, half seller-side (the sources' own approximation, not a hard NAR split) gives ~5–6 buyer-side closings/agent/year — i.e. ~5-6 households a year who are actually moving in. 3.3 Elder-care / in-home senior care — the strongest emotional angle

Why it works: aides are already inside these homes and already absorb tech complaints and scam-call fallout informally, unpaid. Value to them: a vetted referral offloads a liability/time burden they don't want. Value to A777ance: the closest match to the "pest control" positioning — quiet prevention nobody thinks about until it isn't there — plus a household.

Sizing the opportunity: no public, citable figure was found for active client caseloads at any of these five agencies, and none is invented here — that would break the same honesty rule this repo already applies to the "How You Compare" statement benchmark. Treat this as a trust channel, not a volume channel, until real numbers exist. The first call itself should ask the denominator: "how many households do your aides currently serve in [Northampton/Enfield-area towns]?" — once that number exists, apply the same flat 5% conversion as every other channel: caseload × 5% = expected homes, each worth $175 one-time + $20/mo to HQ (Remote tier).

3.6 Insurance agents/brokers — the "risk conversation" channel

Why it works: an independent agent already has a risk conversation with every homeowner at binding and renewal, and there's a real, documented coverage gap that maps closely onto what A777ance reduces. Value to them: a value-add they can raise in a conversation they're already having, without underwriting anything themselves. Value to A777ance: a homeowner talking to their insurance agent is already in a risk-and-liability mindset — a stronger frame to enter on than a cold "pest control" pitch.

What was verified (cited) before using this as a pitch:

  • Real, but a different product than ours: monitored physical security systems (burglar alarms, water-leak sensors) earn genuine discounts — 5–20% off premium, roughly $75–$460/yr on a ~$2,300 policy, gated on 24/7 professional monitoring. Sources: NerdWallet, Policygenius, American Family. This is alarm/leak-sensor monitoring, not network/DNS security — no evidence was found that A777ance itself qualifies for this specific discount, and none is claimed here.
  • Real coverage gap, directly relevant: standard homeowners policies (ISO HO-3/HO-5 forms) do not include cyber coverage by default. Sources: NerdWallet, Forbes Advisor, U.S. News.
  • Real, growing product: homeowners can add a cyber/ID-theft rider (sublimit often $10k–$25k) or a standalone personal cyber policy ($100k–$250k limits, ~$250–$500/yr), and industry material names the driver explicitly: networked smart-home devices (cameras, thermostats, locks, voice assistants) expanding a household's attack surface. Sources: Mercury Insurance, Grange Insurance, Smart Home Security Authority.

What's not verified — say this honestly on the call, don't assume it:

  • Whether any specific carrier credits a monitored home network itself (as opposed to a monitored alarm system) — general search found nothing; ask the broker directly, they know their carriers' underwriting guidelines better than public sources show.
  • Book-of-business size for any of these specific agencies. General search only turned up a wide, mixed-reliability range (roughly 1,000–1,800 policies/year for a solo agent, not broken out by home vs. auto or new vs. renewal) — too noisy to build a table on, unlike the NAR/HOA anchors above. Same treatment as §3.3: get the real number on the call, then apply the flat 5%: new homeowner policies bound/year × 5% = expected referred homes, each worth $175 one-time + $20/mo to HQ (Remote tier).

The honest pitch, given all that: not "get a discount" — "your clients are increasingly buying coverage for a risk category (networked smart-home device compromise) that A777ance directly reduces; worth a mention alongside that conversation, especially right after a claim or a near-miss."

OPERATOR RECRUIT CHANNEL not customers — labor supply

3.4 Local IT / computer-repair shops

Why it works: these shops already have the truck, the in-home trust, and the skill; they're currently billing one ticket at a time with no recurring revenue and no marketing engine. Value to them: joining converts unpredictable ticket income into a flat-due retainer book, plus a portfolio page that markets them instead of them chasing the next job. Value to A777ance: solves the Phase 1 roadmap's explicit need for 1–2 non-founder operators — supply without a hire.

Sizing the opportunity — corrected to the calculator's actual Local-model structure (this replaces an earlier, wrong version of this section that assumed HQ billed the household directly): the $50/mo due is a locked fact (ADR-005) — guaranteed the moment someone joins, flat, and it does not scale with book size. Per the calculator's own footnote, "Alliance HQ never bills the customer directly in this model" — an operator's folded-in customers pay the operator $175 one-time + $20/mo directly, not HQ. So recruiting one operator is worth exactly $50/mo to HQ, whether they bring 5 households or 30. Book size changes the operator's own income and the guild's visible density/proof — not HQ's direct cut.

4. Outreach script skeletons (starting points, tune per conversation)

  • HOA/property manager: "I run a local service that keeps home networks — Wi-Fi, VPN, filtering — quietly maintained, with a monthly proof-of-work report homeowners actually read. Would [board / newsletter] be open to a one-page intro for [community name]? No cost to the HOA — homeowners pay their own membership."
  • Real-estate agent: "I help new homeowners get their network set up and secured in move-in week — one less thing on the closing checklist. Happy to give you a one-pager you can hand buyers, no referral fee expected up front — I'd rather earn the relationship."
  • Elder-care agency: "Your aides are in these homes anyway — a lot of them are dealing with routers that don't work and grandparents who are one scam call away from trouble. I run a managed home-network service built around exactly that — quiet, monthly-verified, and it takes the tech burden off your staff."
  • IT/computer-repair operator recruit: "You're already doing in-home network work one ticket at a time. I've built a guild model — flat $50/mo due, a portfolio dashboard that markets your book of homes, and a matching engine that sends you customers — instead of chasing one-off jobs. Want to see how the economics compare to what you're billing now?"

5. Metrics to track (from the existing roadmap — don't invent new ones)

  • Density: homes per active route in the two lead towns.
  • Referral rate: % of new homes sourced from existing customers.
  • Conversion: % of customers expressing interest in becoming operators.
  • Operator economics: homes per operator, dues coverage.
  • Retention: monthly churn.
  • Channel yield (new this quarter): homes sourced per customer channel (HOA / real-estate / elder-care / insurance), tracked separately from operators recruited via the IT-shop channel and from the organic neighbor-referral rate above (that one isn't a §3 channel at all) — tells you which channel to double down on before Phase 2.

6. What this plan deliberately does not do

  • No paid ads, no billboard, no mass-market spend — liquidity-before-app means spend goes into relationships and proof, not media buys, for these 90 days.
  • No invented cohort data or fabricated "how you compare" benchmarks — the honesty-of-the-kept-document rule holds for marketing collateral too.
  • No claims about pricing beyond the Master Amounts Calculator's three tiers (Self-Service $0+$20/mo; Remote $175+$20/mo; Local $175+$20/mo to the Operator, $50/mo flat due to HQ) — this supersedes the README's older ADR-007 figure, per confirmation 2026-07-15.
